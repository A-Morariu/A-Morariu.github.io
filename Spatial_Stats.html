Spatial Stats

Where did this all start?

In my final year of my undergraduate degree, I took a course in spatial statistics taught by none other than Prof. Daniel Simpson. As someone that’s always asked “what’s the point” to almost everything that I was taught, spatial statistics always provided a very clear answer to that. Every model that we use has a tangible underlying object, disease, species, or thing we are interested in looking at but also takes into account the **location* of these objects which has always been ignored. The assumption that where observations were made doesn’t matter has never sat well with me so this was a breadth of fresh air.

As a result, I created this blog post to showcase any work I’ve done in this field. I want to take this opportunity to mention that spatial statistics is heavily based on Bayesian methods so I recommend familarizing yourself with those before proceedig. My advice is to read Bayesian Data Analysis by Gelman et. all. Further, most models are fit using R-INLA or R-Stan; two statistical packages that are very powerful and very confusing but worth the struggle!

Posterior sampling of random effects using INLA

While working on an air pollution project during my time and St. Michael’s hospital, we were interested in sampling the poster distribution of a random effect within a model. This exercise is broken down into three parts in order to explain the methodology.

  1. Understanding INLA output
  2. Formal mathematical argument for sampling
  3. Implemenation/code

What are you doing INLA

If you’ve fit any model in INLA you know that it outputs a nested list that can be hard to navigate. Fortunately, thanks to Prof. Patrick Brown and his Applied Statistics II we can find some clarity. We know that INLA provides estimates of the posterior distribution of the latent field of the model. The details of how it does this will be omitted as they require solving stochastic partial differential equations and that is a battle for a different day. Let’s begin with an example:

The Bacteria Dataset

The question we are asking is whether or not a given treatment reduces the probability that the bacteria is present in the subject’s body.

The Model

The variables are defined as follows:

  • \(y_{it}\) is the presence of bacteria in individual \(i\) at time \(t\)
  • \(X_{it}\) is the design matrix for the indicator of the week the individual was examined
  • \(U_i\) is the individual level random effect

And the model is structured accordingly:

\[\begin{align} y_{it} &\sim Bernoulli(\lambda_{it}) \\ logit(\lambda_{it}) &= \mu + X_{it} \beta + U_i \\ U_i &\sim N(0, \sigma^2) \end{align}\]

Finally, setting the priors:

\[\begin{align} \mu &\sim N(0, 10^2) \\ \beta &\sim N(0, 3^2 I) \\ \sigma &= 1 \end{align}\]

Now we can go ahead and fit this model using INLA… right? Not quite. Because we have repeated measurements from the same individual (the time component of our data) you no longer have independence between the data points. Furthermore, some people are naturally more succeptible to certain bacterias and don’t have any additional information to highlight those people. As a result, we will restructre our model into a more convenient form for INLA to work with (and luckily for us to understand). From theory on GLMs, specifically the link functions, we know that \(logit(\lambda_{it}) \sim N(\cdot, \cdot)\) resulting in the following equivalent model:

\[\begin{align} y_{it} &\sim Bernoulli(\lambda_{it}) \\ logit(\lambda_{it}) &= \eta_{it} = X_{it} \beta + U_i \\ U_i &\sim N(0, \sigma^2) \end{align}\]

And the reformulated priors: \[\begin{align} \beta_0 &\sim N(0, 10^2) \\ \{\beta_p ; p > 1 \} &\overset{ind}{\sim} N(0, 3^2 I) \\ \sigma &= 1 \end{align}\]

In doing this, the propertie of multivariate normals can be exploited (which is what INLA needs to work it’s magic). The key result here is:

\[\begin{equation} W = (\eta, U, \beta)^T \sim MVN \end{equation}\]

Which allows INLA to create a Gaussian approximation of this latent field. It is worth noting that the the dimensions of the field \(W\).

\[\begin{align} dim(W) &= ncol(\eta) + ncol(U) + ncol(\beta) \\ &= \text{Number of observations} + \text{Number of individuals} + \text{number of treatments} \end{align}\]

The latent field is composed of submatricies of every observation (\(\eta\)), random effects (how many \(U_i\)’s there are), and fixed effects (how many \(\beta\)’s there are) which are in the model. Because we are using the multivariate normal, we need to write our priors in a vectorized form too. \[\begin{align} \eta \vert \beta, U &\sim N(X\beta + AU, \epsilon I) \\ \beta &\sim N(0,\Gamma) \\ U \vert \sigma &\sim N(0, \Sigma_\sigma) \\ \sigma &= 1 \end{align}\]

\[\begin{equation} W \vert \sigma \sim N \left( 0, \begin{pmatrix} A\Sigma_\sigma A^T + X \Gamma X^T & A^T & X^T \\ A & \Sigma_\sigma & 0 \\ X & 0 & \Gamma \end{pmatrix} \right) \end{equation}\]

And this is the estimate which INLA gives us. The latent field can become quite large very quickly since each new data point adds a row and a column to the precision matrix of \(W\) (due to the increase of the dimension of \(\eta\)); however, the precision matrix tends to be quite sparse which makes further computations signifcantly faster.

This is a good opportunity to take a look back at our initial goal: taking posterior samples of the random effects in the model. Knowing the structure of \(W\), it is clear that the we are interested in the submatrix \(U\). All that’s required is to take transform \(W\) by some matrix \(A\) which leaves us with just \(U\).

\[\begin{align} A &= \begin{pmatrix} 0_\eta \\ \hline I \\ \hline 0_\beta \end{pmatrix} \\ 0_\eta &\in \mathbb{R}^{nrow(U) \times ncol(\eta)} \\ I &\in \mathbb{R}^{nrow(U) \times ncol(U)} \\ 0_\beta &\in \mathbb{R}^{nrow(U) \times ncol(\beta)} \end{align}\]

Transforming a mulivariate normal for selective sampling

Unfortunately, sampling \(U\) is not as simple as pulling it out of \(W\). In this section, we show the mathematical procedure for generating a random sample from the same distribution as \(U\). The Cholesky decompositon will be used in order to break down the precision matrix in order to create a random sample from \(U\).

\[\begin{align} W &\sim N(\mu_W, Q_W^{-1}) \\ \Rightarrow A^TW &\sim N(A\mu_W, A^T Q_W^{-1} A) \\ &\sim N(A^T\mu_W, A^T (L_W L_W^T)^{-1} A) \\ &\sim N(A^T\mu_W, A^T (L_W^{-1})^T (L_W)^{-1} A) \\ &\sim N(A^T\mu_W, (L_W^{-1} A)^T ((L_W)^{-1} A)) \\ &\sim N(A^T\mu_W, \widetilde{L}^T \widetilde{L} ) \\ &\sim N(A^T\mu_W, \Sigma_U) \end{align}\]

Taking a random sample from the standard multivariate normal \(Z\) and applying the following transformation we get a sample from the desired distribution

\[\begin{align} A^T \mu_x + \widetilde{L}Z \sim N_p (A^T\mu_x, \Sigma_U) \Leftrightarrow A^T \mu_x + \widetilde{L}Z &\overset{d}{=} U \end{align}\]
